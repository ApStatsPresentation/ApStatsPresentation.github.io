Do Freshman Get More Sleep than Juniors?

By Jeremy Asuncion, Jack Vang, Jay Calunsag, David Escalante

Hypothesis

\( H_0 \): Freshmen get the same amount of sleep as Juniors.

\( H_a \): Freshmen get more sleep than Juniors.

Experimental Design

  • Survey with two questions: Grade Level and Mean Hours of Sleep

  • Four populations were sampled: Freshmen, Sophomores, Juniors, and Seniors

  • Sampling Method:

    • Cluster sample; 9 classes selected randomly using true random number generator

    • Only data from Freshmen and Juniors were kept

    • 50 data were required from both Freshmen and Juniors

  • Surveys with obviously unrealistic results were not considered — for example, no hours of sleep

Difference of Means

\( \mu_f = \) mean hours of sleep for Freshmen.

\( \mu_j = \) mean hours of sleep for Juniors.

\[ \begin{split} &H_0: \mu_f - \mu_j= 0 \\ &\implies \mu_f = \mu_j \end{split} \]

\[ \begin{split} &H_a: \mu_f - \mu_j > 0 \\ &\implies \mu_f > \mu_j \end{split} \]

Assumptions

  • Randomization: Classes were randomly selected using TRG

  • 10% Conditions: The freshmen sample satisfy this conditon if a randomly selected subset of \(n = 50 \) data is selected; the Juniors satisfy this condition

  • Nearly Normal:

  • Independence Conditions: Assume that Juniors and Sophomores are independent, and that each student from each class is independent(That's right! No one has friends!)

\( \displaystyle t = \) \( \displaystyle \frac{\bar{x}_f - \bar{x}_j}{\sqrt{\frac{s_f^2}{n_f} + \frac{s_j^2}{n_j}}} \) \( \displaystyle = \frac{6.71 - 6.39}{\sqrt{\frac{2.16}{49} + \frac{1.99}{48}}} \) \(\displaystyle = 1.53 \)

\( \displaystyle df = \) \( \displaystyle \frac{\left( \frac{s_f^2}{n_f} + \frac{s_j^2}{n_j}\right)^2} {\frac{1}{n_f - 1}\left( \frac{s_f^2}{n_f} \right)^2 + \frac{1}{n_j - 1}\left( \frac{s_j^2}{n_j} \right)^2 } \) \( \displaystyle = \frac{\left( \frac{2.36}{49} + \frac{1.99}{48}\right)^2} {\frac{1}{49 - 1}\left( \frac{2.36}{49} \right)^2 + \frac{1}{48 - 1}\left( \frac{1.99}{48} \right)^2 } \) \( \displaystyle = 95.168 \)

\( \displaystyle p \) \( \displaystyle = P\left( t_{95.168} > 1.53 \right) \)
\( \displaystyle = 1 - \int_{-\infty}^t \left( \frac{\Gamma\left( \frac{df + 1}{2} \right)} {\sqrt{df \cdot \pi} \Gamma\left( \frac{df}{2} \right)} \left( 1 + \frac{x^2}{df} \right)^{-\frac{df + 1}{2}} \right) dx \)
= 1 - 0.935 = 0.065

\( \chi^2 \) Test of Homogeneity

\( H_0 \): The distribution of average sleep times for Freshmen is the same as the distribution of average sleep times for Juniors

\( H_a \): The distribution of average sleep times are not the same

If \( H_a \) is true, we'd have to check the standardized residuals, \( \displaystyle C = \frac{O - E}{\sqrt{E}} \), to see who got more sleep.

  • Data is categorized into two categories:

    • \( \mu \geq 8 \) — required amount of sleep for teenager

    • \( \mu < 8 \) — less than the required amount

  • There are two populations(Freshmen and Juniors) and one variable(Average sleep time), therefore \( \chi^2 \) test of homogeneity is used

\[ df = \left( R - 1 \right)\left( C - 1 \right) = 1 \cdot 1 = 1 \]

Assumptions

  • Counted Data: Since the data was categorized, we have counted data

  • Randomization: A TRG was used to randomly select classes

  • Expected Cell Frequency:

\( \displaystyle \frac{1}{112} \begin{pmatrix} 31 \\ 81 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 64 & 48 \end{pmatrix} \)

\( \displaystyle = \begin{pmatrix} 17.174 & 13.286 \\ 46.286 & 34.714 \end{pmatrix} \)

\( \displaystyle \chi^2 = \) \( \displaystyle \sum_{i = 1}^4 \frac{\left( O_i - E_i \right)^2}{E_i} \) \( \displaystyle = \frac{\left( 21 - 17.174 \right)^2}{17.174} \) \( \displaystyle + \frac{\left( 10 - 13.286 \right)^2}{13.286} \) \( \displaystyle + \frac{\left( 43 - 46.286 \right)^2}{46.286} \) \( \displaystyle + \frac{\left( 38 - 34.714 \right)^2}{34.714} \) \( \displaystyle = 1.967 \)

\( \displaystyle p \) \( \displaystyle = 2P\left( \chi^2 > 1.967 \right) \)
\( \displaystyle = 1 - \int_0^x \left( \frac{x^{df/2 - 1} \operatorname{exp}\left( -\frac{x}{2} \right)} {2^{df/2} \Gamma\left( \frac{df}{2} \right)} \right) dx \)
= 1 - 0.839 = 0.161
Graph

Conclusions

  • We used an \( \alpha = 0.05 \)

  • \( p_t = 0.065 > \alpha = 0.05 \) and \( p_{\chi^2} = 0.161 > \alpha = 0.05 \), so we fail to reject the null hypothesis

WHAT DOES IT MEAN?

  • There isn't enough evidence to suggest Freshmen get more sleep than juniors

  • It appears that both Freshmen and Juniors get the same amount of sleep
